History

The visit of the medina, ancient city, carries in it a special atmosphere, it is the occasion of a journey through time that introduces, step by step, and through a maze with subtle compositions of shadows and lights, in the historical heart of the city of Tunis, a city rich in history and situated on a plan of continuous evolution.

Medina
Certification

Because of its geographical and human importance in terms of its typical architectural style and spatial organization, this city represents an accomplished model of the Arab urban civilization in the Maghreb, and that the international organization of UNESCO has elected to the cultural heritage of humanity.

Founded in 698, it develops its structure throughout the Middle Ages, to the north and to the south, thus dividing itself into a main medina and two suburbs to the north (Bab Souika) and to the south (Bab El Jazira).

Medina in paint
Craft

Today, the medina alone inspires and constitutes a tourist attraction as well as a sentimental attraction, because of its history, its cultural richness and the craftsmanship that remains within its walls, its streets and its doors.

Architectural

The medina of Tunis was in an oval form, surrounded by a continuous wall and dominated by a Kasba, seat of the Almohado-Hafsid government. It is built around the religious centre of the great Zitouna mosque, opening on a wide esplanade serving both as a market, a place for public meetings and a place for military parades.

Aerial View
Plan

The architecture of the medina forms a network similar to a spider web and represents the typical example of the Arab-Muslim city. It is a marvellous illustration of the richness of the old town by its style, which distinguishes it from the modern city with its checkered avenues.

In terms of the architecture of the houses, two specificities emerge. First, the height of the walls masks the interior of the house. Second, the homes reflect very little of the wealth of their owners, unlike the western model where architecture reflected the wealth and social status of the owners.

This discretion which served social peace was most probably inseparable from the double character of the Arab-Muslim tradition: to limit the ostentation and the exhibition of what takes place in the house. The only exception: the entrance doors, whose ornamentation and material were revealing.
